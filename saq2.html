A test runner moves according to the multi-stage displacement-time graph below. Determine the instantaneous velocity at $t = 3.0\text{ s}$ and find the overall average speed across the complete $5.0\text{ s}$ path window.
Model Answer & Graph Solution:
1. Instantaneous Velocity at $t = 3.0\text{ s}$: This time lies on the linear segment from $t = 2.0\text{ s}$ to $t = 4.0\text{ s}$.
$\text{Gradient} = \frac{x_2 - x_1}{t_2 - t_1} = \frac{30.0 - 10.0}{4.0 - 2.0} = \frac{20.0}{2.0} = 10.0\text{ m s}^{-1}$.
2. Average Speed for total path ($t = 0$ to $5\text{ s}$):
Total Distance covered = $10.0\text{ m}$ (first stage) + $20.0\text{ m}$ (second stage) + $10.0\text{ m}$ (final return stage) = $40.0\text{ m}$.
$\text{Average Speed} = \frac{\text{Total Distance}}{\text{Total Time}} = \frac{40.0\text{ m}}{5.0\text{ s}} = 8.00\text{ m s}^{-1}$.

A hockey stick strikes a puck, exerting a variable contact force profile as shown below. Determine the total impulse delivered to the puck and find its exit velocity if its mass is $0.160\text{ kg}$ and it was initially stationary.
Model Answer & Solutions:
The area under a Force-Time graph equals total Impulse.
$\text{Impulse (Area)} = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height} = \frac{1;2} \times 0.080\text{ s} \times 150\text{ N} = 6.00\text{ N s}$.

By the Impulse-Momentum Theorem:
$\text{Impulse} = \Delta p = m(v_f - v_i) \implies 6.00 = 0.160 \times (v_f - 0)$
$v_f = \frac{6.00}{0.160} = 37.5\text{ m s}^{-1}$.
Question 7Module 2 • Free-Body Vector Layouts • 4 Marks
A cargo container is pulled up an inclined structural ramp by an external tension cable. Using the diagram below, resolve the components of the gravitational force parallel and perpendicular to the ramp surface, and calculate the net accelerating force.
Model Answer & Solutions:
Mass $m = 40.0\text{ kg}$, Ramp Angle $\theta = 30^\circ$, Tension $T = 300\text{ N}$.
• Gravity component pulling down parallel to the ramp:
$F_{g,\parallel} = m g \sin(\theta) = 40.0 \times 9.80 \times \sin(30^\circ) = 392 \times 0.5 = 196\text{ N}$ down the ramp.
• Gravity component perpendicular to the ramp:
$F_{g,\perp} = m g \cos(\theta) = 40.0 \times 9.80 \times \cos(30^\circ) = 392 \times 0.866 = 339.5\text{ N}$.

• Net Force acting along the ramp surface line:
$F_{\text{net}} = T - F_{g,\parallel} = 300\text{ N} - 196\text{ N} = +104\text{ N}$ directed up the ramp structure.
Question 8Module 2 • Hooke's Law Linear Plots • 3 Marks
Review the experimental force-extension data plotted for a structural spring. Calculate the spring constant ($k$) using the gradient of the graph, and determine the elastic potential energy stored at an extension of $0.40\text{ m}$.
Model Answer & Solutions:
1. Find the Spring Constant ($k$): Select coordinate point $(0.40\text{ m}, 20\text{ N})$.
$k = \text{Gradient} = \frac{\Delta F}{\Delta x} = \frac{20\text{ N}}{0.40\text{ m}} = 50.0\text{ N m}^{-1}$.

2. Determine the Stored Elastic Potential Energy ($U_s$): This matches the area under the linear curve up to $x = 0.40\text{ m}$.
$U_s = \frac{1}{2} k x^2 = \frac{1}{2} \times 50.0 \times (0.40)^2 = 25.0 \times 0.16 = 4.00\text{ J}$.

Question 13Module 3 • Snell's Ray Trajectories • 3 Marks

• Angle of incidence ($\theta_1$) $= 90^\circ - 30^\circ = 60^\circ$.
• Angle of refraction ($\theta_2$) $= 90^\circ - 55^\circ = 35^\circ$.

Applying Snell's Law ($n_1 \sin\theta_1 = n_2 \sin\theta_2$):
$1.00 \times \sin(60^\circ) = n_2 \times \sin(35^\circ)$
$0.8660 = n_2 \times 0.5736$
$n_2 = \frac{0.8660}{0.5736} = 1.51$.

Question 17Module 4 • Electric Field Mapping • 3 Marks
The canvas diagram below shows two isolated, oppositely charged point sources. Sketch the electric field line configuration produced around this pair, including directional arrows.
Model Answer & Electric Field Vector Mapping:
Electric field lines trace the path a positive test charge would take. Therefore, the field lines must:
1. Begin on the positive charge ($+q$) and curve smoothly to end on the negative charge ($-q$).
2. Enter and leave the surface of each charge perpendicular to its boundary.
3. Never cross or intersect one another.
The arrows must point **away from the positive charge** and **toward the negative charge**.
Question 18Module 4 • Magnetic Solenoid Polarities • 3 Marks
A direct current flows through a wire coil wrapped around an iron core, as shown below. Use the Right-Hand Grip Rule to determine which end of the solenoid acts as the magnetic North pole.
Model Answer & Solutions:
According to the Right-Hand Grip Rule for solenoids, wrap the fingers of your right hand around the coil in the direction of the conventional current flow (which goes up over the front of the core face). Your extended right thumb will then point toward the magnetic North pole.

Following this technique, your thumb points to the **Left Side**. Therefore, the left end of the core acts as the magnetic North Pole, and the right end acts as the South Pole.

Question 22Synoptic Extension • Bridge Loading Force Analysis • 4 Marks
A uniform horizontal support beam measuring $6.0\text{ m}$ in length has a weight of $200\text{ N}$. It is supported at both ends by vertical pillars, $A$ and $B$. A heavy load weighing $600\text{ N}$ is positioned on the beam exactly $2.0\text{ m}$ from Pillar $A$, as illustrated below. Calculate the upward support forces exerted by both pillars to keep the system in equilibrium.
Model Answer & Solutions:
For rotational equilibrium, the sum of the torques around any pivot point must equal zero. Let's choose Pillar $A$ as our pivot point:
$\sum \tau_A = 0 \implies (600\text{ N} \times 2.0\text{ m}) + (200\text{ N} \times 3.0\text{ m}) - (F_B \times 6.0\text{ m}) = 0$
$1200 + 600 = 6.0 \cdot F_B \implies 1800 = 6.0 \cdot F_B \implies F_B = 300\text{ N}$.

For vertical translational equilibrium, the total upward forces must equal the total downward forces:
$F_A + F_B = 600\text{ N} + 200\text{ N} \implies F_A + 300 = 800 \implies F_A = 500\text{ N}$.
Question 23Synoptic Extension • Thermodynamic Phase Change Matrices • 4 Marks
A $0.25\text{ kg}$ sample of an unknown material is heated uniformly. Using the heating curve provided below, determine the specific heat capacity of the material in its solid phase and calculate its specific heat capacity in its liquid phase.
Model Answer & Solutions:
1. Solid Phase Specific Heat Capacity ($c_{\text{solid}}$): Tracks from $Q = 0$ to $Q = 10\text{ kJ}$ ($10000\text{ J}$), over a temperature range of $\Delta T = 20^\circ\text{C}$ to $60^\circ\text{C} = 40^\circ\text{C}$.
$Q = m c \Delta T \implies 10000 = 0.25 \times c_{\text{solid}} \times 40 \implies 10000 = 10 \cdot c_{\text{solid}}$
$c_{\text{solid}} = 1000\text{ J kg}^{-1}\text{ K}^{-1}$.

2. Liquid Phase Specific Heat Capacity ($c_{\text{liquid}}$): Tracks from $Q = 40\text{ kJ}$ to $Q = 70\text{ kJ}$ ($30000\text{ J}$), over a temperature range of $\Delta T = 60^\circ\text{C}$ to $120^\circ\text{C} = 60^\circ\text{C}$.
$Q = m c \Delta T \implies 30000 = 0.25 \times c_{\text{liquid}} \times 60 \implies 30000 = 15 \cdot c_{\text{liquid}}$
$c_{\text{liquid}} = 2000\text{ J kg}^{-1}\text{ K}^{-1}$.
Question 24Synoptic Extension • Mixed Bridge Network Analysis • 4 Marks
For the complex resistor circuit network shown below, calculate the equivalent total resistance of the circuit and determine the current flowing through Resistor 1 ($R_1$).
Model Answer & Solutions:
1. Calculate the parallel branch resistance ($R_p$): Contains $R_2 = 10\ \Omega$ and $R_3 = 10\ \Omega$ in parallel.
$\frac{1}{R_p} = \frac{1}{10} + \frac{1}{10} = \frac{2}{10} \implies R_p = 5.0\ \Omega$.

$I_{\text{total}} = \frac{V}{R_{\text{total}}} = \frac{24.0\text{ V}}{12.0\ \Omega} = 2.00\text{ A}$.
Question 25Synoptic Extension • Vector Field Superposition • 4 Marks
Two identical positive point charges, $q_1$ and $q_2$, are fixed in space as shown in the diagram. Sketch the net electric field line configuration for this system, and locate the point where the net electric field strength is exactly zero.
Model Answer & Solutions:
• Field Line Interaction: Because both charges are positive, their electric field lines bend away from each other, creating a repulsive pattern. All arrows must point outwards away from the charges.

• Neutral Zone Location: Since the two charges are equal in magnitude, their opposing electric field vectors cancel out perfectly at the exact **geometric midpoint** between them. At this location, the net electric field strength is $E_{\text{net}} = 0$, creating a neutral point with no field lines passing through it.
