Module 1: Kinematics (Questions 1-5)
Question 1Module 1 • Explanation Based • 3 Marks
Explain how it is entirely possible for an accelerating vehicle to possess an instantaneous vector velocity of zero. Provide a real-world vertical trajectory example to justify your answer.
Model Answer & Criteria Matrix:
Acceleration represents the *rate of change* of velocity ($a = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t}$), not the value of velocity itself. When an object thrown vertically upward reaches the apex of its path, its instantaneous velocity transitions from upward to downward, passing precisely through $0\text{ m s}^{-1}$. However, gravity continuously exerts a net downward force, yielding a constant acceleration of $9.8\text{ m s}^{-2}$ downward at that exact moment.

Marking Rubric:
• 3 Marks: Clear definition of acceleration vs velocity with valid apex physics scenario.
• 2 Marks: Mentions apex velocity zero but lacks robust acceleration tracking definition.
• 1 Mark: Fragmented understanding of zero velocity conditions.
Question 2Module 1 • Scientific Skills • 3 Marks
A physics group utilizes a ticker-timer producing dot patterns every $0.02\text{ s}$ to calculate tracking acceleration. Distinguish clearly between the terms accuracy and reliability within the framework of this specific kinematic recording setup.
Model Answer & Criteria Matrix:
Accuracy refers to how closely the calculated experimental acceleration matches the accepted true reference value ($9.80\text{ m s}^{-2}$). It is improved by minimizing systematic errors, such as friction on the tape or timing calibration issues. Reliability denotes the consistency or reproducibility of the data trails across multiple repeated runs. It is verified by repeating trials under identical conditions and looking for minimal spread in the results.

Marking Rubric:
• 3 Marks: Clear distinction linking each term perfectly to the ticker-timer apparatus components.
• 2 Marks: Defines both terms accurately but lacks explicit technical link to the experiment.
• 1 Mark: Confuses accuracy with reliability definitions.
Question 3Module 1 • Mathematical Matrix • 4 Marks
A performance sports car starts from rest at a coordinate datum position and accelerates uniformly along a straight drag strip at a rate of $a = 4.50\text{ m s}^{-2}$ for exactly $t_1 = 6.00\text{ s}$. The driver then immediately applies brakes, generating a constant deceleration profile to bring the vehicle safely to rest over a displacement of $\Delta x = 50.0\text{ m}$. Calculate the total time elapsed from initial launch to final rest.
Model Answer & Solutions:
Step 1: Determine peak velocity at the end of the first phase:
$v = u + at_1 = 0 + (4.50 \times 6.00) = 27.0\text{ m s}^{-1}$

Step 2: Track braking phase to rest to compute $t_2$:
We know $v_i = 27.0\text{ m s}^{-1}$, $v_f = 0$, and $\Delta x = 50.0\text{ m}$. Using average velocity mechanics:
$\Delta x = \left(\frac{v_i + v_f}{2}\right)t_2 \implies 50.0 = \left(\frac{27.0 + 0}{2}\right)t_2 \implies 50.0 = 13.5 \cdot t_2 \implies t_2 = 3.70\text{ s}$

Step 3: Total time:
$t_{\text{total}} = t_1 + t_2 = 6.00 + 3.70 = 9.70\text{ s}$.
Question 4Module 1 • Mathematical Matrix • 3 Marks
A deep-space tracking vector reveals a satellite moving at an initial vector velocity of $150\text{ m s}^{-1}$ due East. Exactly $25.0\text{ s}$ later, corrections output a final vector velocity of $200\text{ m s}^{-1}$ due North. Compute the average acceleration vector magnitude over this window.
Model Answer & Solutions:
The vector change in velocity is $\Delta\vec{v} = \vec{v}_f - \vec{v}_i = \vec{v}_f + (-\vec{v}_i)$. This maps a right triangle with vectors $200\text{ m s}^{-1}$ North and $150\text{ m s}^{-1}$ West.
$|\Delta\vec{v}| = \sqrt{200^2 + 150^2} = 250\text{ m s}^{-1}$.

Average acceleration magnitude:
$a_{\text{avg}} = \frac{|\Delta\vec{v}|}{\Delta t} = \frac{250\text{ m s}^{-1}}{25.0\text{ s}} = 10.0\text{ m s}^{-2}$.
Question 5Module 1 • Mathematical Matrix • 3 Marks
An asset drops from rest from a vertical platform height of $80.0\text{ m}$. Simultaneously, an interceptor projectile is fired vertically upward from baseline ground level with an initial launch speed of $u_0$. Calculate the required value of $u_0$ for the items to collide exactly halfway down ($y = 40.0\text{ m}$). Use $g = 9.80\text{ m s}^{-2}$.
Model Answer & Solutions:
Step 1: Solve for falling asset transit timeline to the midpoint:
$\Delta y_{\text{down}} = \frac{1}{2}gt^2 \implies 40.0 = 4.90 \cdot t^2 \implies t = \sqrt{\frac{40.0}{4.90}} = 2.857\text{ s}$

Step 2: Frame the interceptor ascending displacement equation over the same time:
$\Delta y_{\text{up}} = u_0 t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2 \implies 40.0 = u_0(2.857) - 4.90(2.857)^2$
Notice $4.90(2.857)^2 = 40.0$, thus: $40.0 = u_0(2.857) - 40.0 \implies 80.0 = u_0(2.857) \implies u_0 = \frac{80.0}{2.857} = 28.0\text{ m s}^{-1}$.
Module 2: Dynamics (Questions 6-10)
Question 6Module 2 • Explanation Based • 3 Marks
Explain the operation of an automotive seatbelt system during a severe frontal collision, justifying its structural function explicitly with reference to Newton's First Law of Motion.
Model Answer & Criteria Matrix:
Newton's First Law (Law of Inertia) states that an object maintains its state of rest or constant linear velocity unless acted upon by an external net force. When a car stops suddenly in a collision, the passenger possesses independent forward velocity and continues moving forward due to inertia. The seatbelt provides the necessary external unbalanced force to decelerate the passenger at the same rate as the vehicle cabin, preventing dangerous secondary impact contact.

Question 8Module 2 • Mathematical Matrix • 4 Marks
A $12.0\text{ kg}$ tracking mass is placed on a flat horizontal table with a coefficient of kinetic friction $\mu_k = 0.25$. An inextensible wire line runs from this block over a frictionless corner pulley to a suspended hanging mass of $8.0\text{ kg}$. Calculate the resulting systemic acceleration of the coupled configuration when released from rest.
Model Answer & Solutions:
Let the system total mass $M_{\text{total}} = 12.0 + 8.0 = 20.0\text{ kg}$.

Step 1: Calculate the opposing kinetic friction force on the table:
$F_f = \mu_k \cdot N = \mu_k \cdot m_1 g = 0.25 \times 12.0 \times 9.80 = 29.4\text{ N}$

Step 2: Calculate the driving weight force from the hanging mass:
$F_{\text{drive}} = m_2 g = 8.0 \times 9.80 = 78.4\text{ N}$

Step 3: Apply Newton's Second Law to the coupled system:
$F_{\text{net}} = F_{\text{drive}} - F_f = 78.4 - 29.4 = 49.0\text{ N}$
$a = \frac{F_{\text{net}}}{M_{\text{total}}} = \frac{49.0\text{ N}}{20.0\text{ kg}} = 2.45\text{ m s}^{-2}$.
Question 9Module 2 • Mathematical Matrix • 3 Marks
A high-velocity projectile massing $m = 0.040\text{ kg}$ tracking at $350\text{ m s}^{-1}$ embeds into a stationary ballistic block of mass $M = 2.46\text{ kg}$. If the block is suspended freely like a pendulum, calculate the initial common velocity profile of the system immediately following the impact.
Model Answer & Solutions:
Using the Conservation of Momentum formula for a perfectly inelastic collision:
$m v_1 + M v_2 = (m + M) v_f$
$(0.040 \times 350) + 0 = (0.040 + 2.46) \cdot v_f$
$14.0 = 2.50 \cdot v_f \implies v_f = \frac{14.0}{2.50} = 5.60\text{ m s}^{-1}$.
Question 10Module 2 • Mathematical Matrix • 3 Marks
An electric mining winch reels in a $250\text{ kg}$ containment unit vertically upwards from a shaft bottom, covering $30.0\text{ m}$ at a uniform velocity over exactly $15.0\text{ s}$. Compute the absolute power output required by the winch motor assembly.
Model Answer & Solutions:
Because the load shifts at a uniform velocity, the tension force equals the gravitational weight force:
$F = mg = 250 \times 9.80 = 2450\text{ N}$

Work done lifting the unit:
$W = F \cdot d = 2450 \times 30.0 = 73500\text{ J}$

Power requirements:
$P = \frac{W}{t} = \frac{73500\text{ J}}{15.0\text{ s}} = 4900\text{ W}$ (or $4.90\text{ kW}$).
Module 3: Waves and Thermodynamics (Questions 11-15)
Question 11Module 3 • Explanation Based • 3 Marks
Explain the foundational structural conditions that govern the generation of a standing wave inside a physical medium, referencing the roles of superposition and reflection.
Model Answer & Criteria Matrix:
A standing wave is formed when two waves of identical frequency, wavelength, and amplitude travel in opposite directions through the same medium. This usually happens when a continuous wave train reflects off a boundary. As these counter-propagating waves pass through each other, they undergo continuous **superposition**. This interference creates a stable pattern of nodes (points of zero amplitude due to destructive interference) and antinodes (points of maximum amplitude due to constructive interference).

Marking Rubric:
• 3 Marks: Identifies identical wave criteria, reflection origins, and maps superposition perfectly to nodes/antinodes.
• 2 Marks: Explains wave overlap but fails to explicitly connect it to boundary reflection conditions.
• 1 Mark: Outlines basic definitions of standing waves without mentioning interference mechanics.

A sub-surface sound pulse targets the ocean floor vertically, receiving a return echo exactly $t = 3.40\text{ s}$ later. If the bulk modulus characteristics of seawater yield an acoustic wave speed of $1480\text{ m s}^{-1}$ with an operating frequency of $f = 25.0\text{ kHz}$, calculate the total tracking depth and the physical wavelength of the signal.
Model Answer & Solutions:
Step 1: Calculate total one-way depth tracking distance:
The total echo time represents round-trip travel. The one-way time is $t_{\text{one-way}} = \frac{3.40}{2} = 1.70\text{ s}$.
$\text{Depth} = v \cdot t_{\text{one-way}} = 1480\text{ m s}^{-1} \times 1.70\text{ s} = 2516\text{ m}$.

Question 14Module 3 • Mathematical Matrix • 3 Marks
A laser path traveling inside a dense core plastic medium meets a planar boundary layer to air ($n_{\text{air}} = 1.00$). Telemetry shows the total internal reflection critical boundary cut-off tracks exactly at an angle of incidence of $\theta_c = 38.5^\circ$. Calculate the refractive index of the plastic medium.
Model Answer & Solutions:
Using Snell's Law configuration for critical angle bounds:
$\sin(\theta_c) = \frac{n_2}{n_1}$ where $n_2 = n_{\text{air}} = 1.00$
$\sin(38.5^\circ) = \frac{1.00}{n_{\text{plastic}}} \implies 0.6225 = \frac{1.00}{n_{\text{plastic}}}$
$n_{\text{plastic}} = \frac{1.00}{0.6225} = 1.61$.
Question 15Module 3 • Mathematical Matrix • 3 Marks
Calculate the total energy required to heat a $0.150\text{ kg}$ block of pure ice initially at $-10.0^\circ\text{C}$ to fully melted liquid water at $+20.0^\circ\text{C}$. Let $c_{\text{ice}} = 2100\text{ J kg}^{-1}\text{ K}^{-1}$, $L_f = 3.34 \times 10^5\text{ J kg}^{-1}$, and $c_{\text{water}} = 4186\text{ J kg}^{-1}\text{ K}^{-1}$.
Model Answer & Solutions:
The process requires three consecutive thermal stages:
1. Heat ice to $0^\circ\text{C}$: $Q_1 = m c_{\text{ice}} \Delta T = 0.150 \times 2100 \times 10 = 3150\text{ J}$
2. Melt ice to water: $Q_2 = m L_f = 0.150 \times (3.34 \times 10^5) = 50100\text{ J}$
3. Heat liquid water to $20^\circ\text{C}$: $Q_3 = m c_{\text{water}} \Delta T = 0.150 \times 4186 \times 20 = 12558\text{ J}$

Total thermal energy needed:
$Q_{\text{total}} = 3150 + 50100 + 12558 = 65808\text{ J}$ (or $65.8\text{ kW}$).
Module 4: Electricity and Magnetism (Questions 16-20)
Question 16Module 4 • Explanation Based • 3 Marks
Describe the movement of free electrons within a solid metallic wire lattice when a constant DC potential difference is applied across its ends, referencing the concept of drift velocity.
Model Answer & Criteria Matrix:
In a metallic conductor, free conduction electrons move in random high-speed paths, colliding continuously with the fixed positive ions of the crystal lattice. When a DC potential difference is applied, an electric field is established through the wire. This field exerts an electrostatic force that superimposes a slow, net directional movement on the electrons toward the positive terminal. This average net directional velocity is known as the **drift velocity**, and it is much slower than the instantaneous speed of the random thermal motion.

Marking Rubric:
• 3 Marks: Connects random thermal velocity, electric field force, and drift velocity mechanics accurately.
• 2 Marks: Mentions drift velocity towards the positive terminal but fails to contrast it with random thermal motion.
• 1 Mark: States that electrons flow through the wire without explaining the motion profile.
Question 17Module 4 • Scientific Skills • 3 Marks
A lab group measures the potential drop across a resistor using a traditional analog voltmeter. Explain how to configure the meter within the circuit topology to protect the validity of the recorded resistance data.
Model Answer & Criteria Matrix:
To measure the true potential difference across a component without altering the circuit's operation, the voltmeter must be connected in **parallel** with that component. Additionally, the voltmeter must have a very high internal resistance. This design ensures that minimal current is diverted away from the main branch through the meter, preserving the original current and voltage distribution across the test network.

Marking Rubric:

• 2 Marks: States parallel placement but lacks an analysis of internal resistance mechanics.

An electrical device runs safely on a $240\text{ V}$ grid connection, drawing a stable current of $I = 6.25\text{ A}$ over an operational window of exactly $2.00\text{ hours}$. Calculate the total electrical energy transferred through the system components.

Question 19Module 4 • Mathematical Matrix • 3 Marks
Two long, straight parallel wires are separated by a distance of $r = 12.0\text{ cm}$. Wire 1 carries a current of $I_1 = 8.0\text{ A}$ pointing upwards, while Wire 2 carries a current of $I_2 = 15.0\text{ A}$ pointing downwards. Calculate the net magnetic field magnitude at the midpoint between the two wires. ($\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7}\text{ T m A}^{-1}$).
Model Answer & Solutions:
The midpoint is located at a distance of $r = 0.060\text{ m}$ from each wire.
Using the Right-Hand Grip Rule: Wire 1 (current up) generates a magnetic field pointing *into the page* at the midpoint. Wire 2 (current down) also generates a magnetic field pointing *into the page* at the midpoint. Because both fields point in the same direction, their magnitudes add together.

$B_1 = \frac{\mu_0 I_1}{2\pi r} = \frac{(4\pi \times 10^{-7}) \times 8.0}{2\pi \times 0.060} = 2.67 \times 10^{-5}\text{ T}$
$B_2 = \frac{\mu_0 I_2}{2\pi r} = \frac{(4\pi \times 10^{-7}) \times 15.0}{2\pi \times 0.060} = 5.00 \times 10^{-5}\text{ T}$

$B_{\text{net}} = B_1 + B_2 = 2.67 \times 10^{-5} + 5.00 \times 10^{-5} = 7.67 \times 10^{-5}\text{ T}$.
Question 20Module 4 • Mathematical Matrix • 3 Marks
A uniform magnetic core creates a field density of $B = 0.045\text{ T}$ oriented straight out of the page plane. A straight wire segment of length $L = 0.15\text{ m}$ lies in the plane of the page and carries a current of $I = 6.0\text{ A}$. Calculate the magnetic force acting on the wire.
Model Answer & Solutions:
The magnetic force on a current-carrying wire is given by:
$F = B I L \sin(\theta)$
Since the magnetic field is perpendicular to the page and the wire lies flat in the page plane, the angle of interaction is exactly $\theta = 90^\circ$ ($\sin(90^\circ) = 1$).

$F = 0.045\text{ T} \times 6.0\text{ A} \times 0.15\text{ m} \times 1 = 0.0405\text{ N}$.
Section 2E: High-Tier Synoptic Extension (Questions 21-25)
Question 21Synoptic extension • Kinematics & Dynamics • 4 Marks
A $5.0\text{ kg}$ tracking drone starting from rest activates its thrusters, experiencing a net force profile given over time by $F(t) = 3.0t + 2.0$. Calculate the velocity achieved by the drone at $t = 4.0\text{ s}$ using impulse-momentum mechanics.
Model Answer & Solutions:
The total impulse delivered equals the area under the force-time graph from $t = 0$ to $t = 4.0\text{ s}$.
At $t = 0$, $F = 2.0\text{ N}$. At $t = 4.0\text{ s}$, $F = 3.0(4.0) + 2.0 = 14.0\text{ N}$.
Since the force function is linear, the area forms a trapezoid:
$\text{Impulse} = \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}(F_{\text{initial}} + F_{\text{final}}) \cdot \Delta t = \frac{1}{2}(2.0 + 14.0) \times 4.0 = 32.0\text{ N s}$

Using the impulse-momentum theorem ($\text{Impulse} = \Delta p = m \Delta v$):
$32.0 = 5.0 \times (v_f - 0) \implies v_f = \frac{32.0}{5.0} = 6.40\text{ m s}^{-1}$.
Question 22Synoptic Extension • Energy Conversion Waves • 4 Marks
A $0.200\text{ kg}$ structural block attached to a horizontal spring oscillates in simple harmonic motion across a frictionless surface. If the wave energy profile shows the system has a maximum speed of $4.00\text{ m s}^{-1}$ and the spring has a stiffness constant of $k = 50.0\text{ N m}$, determine the physical amplitude of the oscillation.
Model Answer & Solutions:
By the Law of Conservation of Energy, the maximum kinetic energy (at equilibrium) equals the maximum potential energy stored in the spring (at maximum displacement/amplitude):
$\frac{1}{2} m (v_{\text{max}})^2 = \frac{1}{2} k A^2$

Substitute the known values into the equation:
$0.200 \times (4.00)^2 = 50.0 \times A^2$
$0.200 \times 16.0 = 50.0 \times A^2 \implies 3.20 = 50.0 \cdot A^2$
$A^2 = \frac{3.20}{50.0} = 0.064 \implies A = \sqrt{0.064} = 0.253\text{ m}$ (or $25.3\text{ cm}$).
Question 23Synoptic Extension • Electricity & Thermal Fields • 4 Marks
An electric immersion heater element running on a $12.0\text{ V}$ DC source is placed inside an insulated beaker containing $0.300\text{ kg}$ of pure water. If the water temperature rises by $\Delta T = 8.00^\circ\text{C}$ over exactly $t = 10.0\text{ minutes}$, calculate the electrical resistance of the heating element. ($c_{\text{water}} = 4186\text{ J kg}^{-1}\text{ K}^{-1}$).
Model Answer & Solutions:
Step 1: Calculate the thermal energy absorbed by the water sample:
$Q = m c \Delta T = 0.300 \times 4186 \times 8.00 = 10046.4\text{ J}$

Step 2: Determine the required electrical power input ($t = 600\text{ s}$):
$P = \frac{Q}{t} = \frac{10046.4\text{ J}}{600\text{ s}} = 16.744\text{ W}$

Step 3: Relate power to voltage and solve for resistance ($R$):
$P = \frac{V^2}{R} \implies 16.744 = \frac{12.0^2}{R} \implies R = \frac{144}{16.744} = 8.60\ \Omega$.
Question 24Synoptic Extension • Circuit Transformation networks • 4 Marks
A complex parallel circuit network consists of three separate resistors connected in parallel ($R_1 = 10\ \Omega$, $R_2 = 20\ \Omega$, and $R_3 = 30\ \Omega$). This entire parallel assembly is connected in series with a fourth resistor, $R_4 = 5.45\ \Omega$. Calculate the total current drawn from a $12.0\text{ V}$ ideal battery connected across the system.
Model Answer & Solutions:
Step 1: Calculate the equivalent resistance of the parallel branch block ($R_p$):
$\frac{1}{R_p} = \frac{1}{10} + \frac{1}{20} + \frac{1}{30} = \frac{6 + 3 + 2}{60} = \frac{11}{60} \implies R_p = \frac{60}{11} \approx 5.454\ \Omega$

Step 2: Add the series resistor component ($R_4$) to find total resistance:
$R_{\text{total}} = R_p + R_4 = 5.454\ \Omega + 5.45\ \Omega = 10.904\ \Omega$

Step 3: Apply Ohm's Law to find the total circuit current:
$I = \frac{V}{R_{\text{total}}} = \frac{12.0\text{ V}}{10.904\ \Omega} = 1.10\text{ A}$.
Question 25Synoptic Extension • Field Balance Mechanics • 4 Marks
A small sphere carrying a net positive charge of $q = +2.50 \times 10^{-6}\text{ C}$ and mass $m = 4.00 \times 10^{-3}\text{ kg}$ is suspended at rest between two large horizontal charged parallel plates. Determine the strength and direction of the electric field required to keep the sphere balanced in mid-air against gravity.
Model Answer & Solutions:
For the sphere to remain suspended at rest, the upward electrostatic force must exactly balance the downward gravitational weight force:
$F_e = F_g \implies q E = m g$

Substitute the values and solve for electric field strength ($E$):
$(2.50 \times 10^{-6}) \cdot E = (4.00 \times 10^{-3}) \times 9.80$
$(2.50 \times 10^{-6}) \cdot E = 3.92 \times 10^{-2}$
$E = \frac{3.92 \times 10^{-2}}{2.50 \times 10^{-6}} = 15680\text{ N C}^{-1}$ (or $\text{V m}^{-1}$).

Direction: Because the charge is positive, the electrostatic force points in the same direction as the electric field. To counter gravity, the force must point upward, meaning the **electric field must point straight upward**.
