Exemplar 1: Complex Thermodynamic Mixture with Latent Phases

A $0.250\,\text{kg}$ block of pure ice at $-15.0^\circ\text{C}$ is placed into an uninsulated aluminum calorimeter container of mass $0.180\,\text{kg}$ containing $0.550\,\text{kg}$ of liquid water at $45.0^\circ\text{C}$. The system is sealed. Concurrently, a heating coil delivers electrical thermal energy at a stable rate of $P = 45.0\,\text{W}$ into the mixture for exactly $12.0\,\text{minutes}$. Assuming negligible thermal leakage to the environment, derive the final stable temperature and phase configuration of the contents.
Given constants: $c_{\text{ice}} = 2100\,\text{J/kg}^\circ\text{C}$, $c_{\text{water}} = 4186\,\text{J/kg}^\circ\text{C}$, $c_{\text{Al}} = 900\,\text{J/kg}^\circ\text{C}$, $L_f = 3.34 \times 10^5\,\text{J/kg}$.

Detailed Analytical Path:

Step 1: Compute absolute external energy added by the element ($Q_{\text{in}}$):

$$Q_{\text{in}} = \text{Power} \times \Delta t = 45.0\,\text{W} \times (12.0 \times 60\,\text{s}) = 32,400\,\text{J}$$

Step 2: Determine thermal energy required to bring ice to its transition gateway ($0^\circ\text{C}$):

$$Q_1 = m_{\text{ice}} c_{\text{ice}} \Delta T_1 = 0.250 \times 2100 \times (0 - (-15)) = 7,875\,\text{J}$$

Step 3: Determine energy required to fully liquefy the ice mass at equilibrium:

$$Q_2 = m_{\text{ice}} L_f = 0.250 \times (3.34 \times 10^5) = 83,500\,\text{J}$$ $$\text{Total energy needed to convert ice to water at } 0^\circ\text{C} = 7,875 + 83,500 = 91,375\,\text{J}$$

Step 4: Determine energy released by the water and aluminum cooling down to $0^\circ\text{C}$:

$$Q_{\text{release}} = (m_{\text{water}}c_{\text{water}} + m_{\text{Al}}c_{\text{Al}})(45.0 - 0)$$ $$Q_{\text{release}} = (0.550 \times 4186 + 0.180 \times 900) \times 45.0 = (2302.3 + 162) \times 45.0 = 2464.3 \times 45.0 = 110,893.5\,\text{J}$$

Step 5: Apply Conservation of Energy Architecture:

$$\Sigma Q = 0 \implies Q_{\text{in}} + Q_{\text{release}} = Q_1 + Q_2 + Q_{\text{final-heating}}$$ $$32,400 + 110,893.5 = 7,875 + 83,500 + Q_{\text{final-heating}}$$ $$143,293.5 = 91,375 + Q_{\text{final-heating}} \implies Q_{\text{final-heating}} = 51,918.5\,\text{J}$$ Since $Q_{\text{final-heating}} > 0$, the ice melts completely, and the unified thermal mass elevates to temperature $T_f$: $$Q_{\text{final-heating}} = (m_{\text{ice}} + m_{\text{water}})c_{\text{water}} T_f + m_{\text{Al}}c_{\text{Al}} T_f$$ $$51,918.5 = \left[ (0.250 + 0.550) \times 4186 + 0.180 \times 900 \right] \times T_f$$ $$51,918.5 = [3348.8 + 162] \times T_f = 3510.8 \times T_f$$ $$T_f = \frac{51,918.5}{3510.8} \approx 14.79^\circ\text{C}$$

Final State: $0.800\,\text{kg}$ of pure liquid water, $0.180\,\text{kg}$ of aluminum container, stabilized cleanly at $14.79^\circ\text{C}$.

Exemplar 2: Double-Source Wave Interference and Spatial Geometry

Two point sources, $S_1$ and $S_2$, fluctuate completely in-phase to create identical coherent sound waves with a frequency of $680\,\text{Hz}$ within an ambient room speed of $v = 340\,\text{m/s}$. A linear track runs parallel to the axis joining $S_1$ and $S_2$ at a baseline displacement distance of $4.00\,\text{m}$. A detector starts on this track directly opposite the midpoint of the two sources and moves laterally. If the spatial spacing between the two point sources is exactly $1.50\,\text{m}$, solve algebraically for the absolute lateral distance the detector must travel to record its very first local amplitude minima (total destructive interference node).

Detailed Analytical Path:

Step 1: Calculate the wavelength ($\lambda$) of the acoustic disturbances:

$$v = f\lambda \implies \lambda = \frac{340\,\text{m/s}}{680\,\text{Hz}} = 0.50\,\text{m}$$

Step 2: Define geometric criteria for structural destructive node 1:

$$\text{Path Difference } (\Delta L) = |S_2 P - S_1 P| = \frac{\lambda}{2} = \frac{0.50}{2} = 0.25\,\text{m}$$

Step 3: Map spatial positions algebraically:

Let the axis center be $(0,0)$. $S_1$ resides at $(-0.75, 0)$ and $S_2$ resides at $(+0.75, 0)$. The detector's track line follows $y = 4.00\,\text{m}$. A moving node position $P$ is denoted as $(x, 4.00)$. $$S_1 P = \sqrt{(x + 0.75)^2 + 4.00^2}, \quad S_2 P = \sqrt{(x - 0.75)^2 + 4.00^2}$$ $$\sqrt{(x + 0.75)^2 + 16} - \sqrt{(x - 0.75)^2 + 16} = 0.25$$

Step 4: Use NESA-approved algebraic expansions (avoiding small-angle approximations for raw geometric accuracy):

$$\sqrt{(x + 0.75)^2 + 16} = \sqrt{(x - 0.75)^2 + 16} + 0.25$$ Squaring both sides cleanly yields: $$(x + 0.75)^2 + 16 = (x - 0.75)^2 + 16 + 0.50\sqrt{(x - 0.75)^2 + 16} + 0.0625$$ $$x^2 + 1.5x + 0.5625 = x^2 - 1.5x + 0.5625 + 0.50\sqrt{(x - 0.75)^2 + 16} + 0.0625$$ $$3.0x - 0.0625 = 0.50\sqrt{(x - 0.75)^2 + 16}$$ Multiply entirely by 2 to clear fractions: $$6.0x - 0.125 = \sqrt{(x - 0.75)^2 + 16}$$ Square both sides a final time: $$36x^2 - 1.5x + 0.015625 = x^2 - 1.5x + 0.5625 + 16$$ $$35x^2 = 16.546875 \implies x^2 \approx 0.472768 \implies x \approx 0.688\,\text{m}$$ The lateral shifting distance required along the monitor track is exactly $0.69\,\text{m}$.

Exemplar 3: Thermal Expansion and Structural Stress Integration

An industrial steel track bridge member ($\alpha = 12 \times 10^{-6}\,\text{K}^{-1}$, Young's Modulus $Y = 2.0 \times 10^{11}\,\text{Pa}$) measures exactly $15.000\,\text{m}$ at an ambient baseline temperature of $12.0^\circ\text{C}$. It is anchored between two immoveable, rigid concrete abutments with an engineered safety expansion gap of exactly $2.5\,\text{mm}$ left open at one end. Calculate the precise temperature at which the steel member expands to bridge the gap completely and subsequently builds an internal compressive stress of magnitude $\sigma = 8.0 \times 10^7\,\text{Pa}$.

Detailed Analytical Path:

Step 1: Determine temperature change $\Delta T_1$ to close the open physical gap:

$$\Delta L_{\text{gap}} = 2.5\,\text{mm} = 2.5 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{m}$$ $$\Delta L = \alpha L_0 \Delta T_1 \implies 2.5 \times 10^{-3} = (12 \times 10^{-6})(15.000)\Delta T_1$$ $$2.5 \times 10^{-3} = 1.8 \times 10^{-4} \Delta T_1 \implies \Delta T_1 = \frac{2.5 \times 10^{-3}}{1.8 \times 10^{-4}} = 13.89^\circ\text{C}$$

Step 2: Determine temperature change $\Delta T_2$ required to build target thermal stress:

Once structural containment blocks further free elongation, any latent virtual expansion leads directly to stress: $$\text{Strain } \epsilon = \frac{\Delta L_{\text{virtual}}}{L_0} = \alpha \Delta T_2$$ $$\text{Stress } \sigma = Y \epsilon = Y \alpha \Delta T_2$$ $$8.0 \times 10^7 = (2.0 \times 10^{11})(12 \times 10^{-6})\Delta T_2$$ $$8.0 \times 10^7 = (2.4 \times 10^6)\Delta T_2 \implies \Delta T_2 = \frac{8.0 \times 10^7}{2.4 \times 10^6} = 33.33^\circ\text{C}$$

Step 3: Combine total thermal path additions:

$$\Delta T_{\text{total}} = \Delta T_1 + \Delta T_2 = 13.89 + 33.33 = 47.22^\circ\text{C}$$ $$T_{\text{final}} = T_0 + \Delta T_{\text{total}} = 12.0^\circ\text{C} + 47.22^\circ\text{C} = 59.22^\circ\text{C}$$ The temperature must reach $59.2^\circ\text{C}$.

Exemplar 4: Closed-Pipe Harmonics & Acoustic Boundary Shifts

An air column inside an adjustable pipe closed at one single end is excited by an adjustable signal generator. At room temperature ($v_{\text{sound}} = 344\,\text{m/s}$), consecutive structural resonances are clearly detected at column lengths of $l_1 = 0.215\,\text{m}$ and $l_2 = 0.430\,\text{m}$ for a fixed source frequency. Analyze the physics of this tube system to deduce the generator's operating frequency, and solve for any systemic end-correction adjustments ($\delta$) influencing the air column boundary.

Detailed Analytical Path:

Step 1: Evaluate structural harmonic criteria for a closed pipe system:

For a pipe closed at one end, consecutive resonant physical structures occur at odd fractional components: $$L_{\text{effective}} = L_{\text{physical}} + \delta$$ Consecutive resonant boundaries satisfy: $$L_{n+1} - L_n = \frac{\lambda}{2}$$ Given $l_2 - l_1 = 0.430 - 0.215 = 0.215\,\text{m}$, we compute: $$\frac{\lambda}{2} = 0.215\,\text{m} \implies \lambda = 0.430\,\text{m}$$

Step 2: Solve for source frequency ($f$):

$$v = f\lambda \implies f = \frac{344\,\text{m/s}}{0.430\,\text{m}} = 800\,\text{Hz}$$

Step 3: Deduce the harmonic modes and evaluate end correction ($\delta$):

Let's test the primary fundamental length condition ($n=1$ mode): $$l_1 + \delta = \frac{\lambda}{4} = \frac{0.430}{4} = 0.1075\,\text{m}$$ However, $l_1 = 0.215\,\text{m}$, which exceeds $\frac{\lambda}{4}$. Therefore, $l_1$ must match the next resonant mode ($n=3$, the 1st overtone): $$l_1 + \delta = \frac{3\lambda}{4} = 3 \times 0.1075 = 0.3225\,\text{m}$$ $$\delta = 0.3225 - 0.215 = 0.1075\,\text{m}$$ Let's verify with $l_2$ ($n=5$ mode): $$l_2 + \delta = \frac{5\lambda}{4} = 5 \times 0.1075 = 0.5375\,\text{m}$$ $$\delta = 0.5375 - 0.430 = 0.1075\,\text{m}$$ The frequency is exactly $800\,\text{Hz}$ and the end correction is $0.1075\,\text{m}$.

Exemplar 5: Multi-Layer Snell's Law and Refractive Limit Matrices

A light beam enters a three-layered transparent crystalline block structure. Layer 1 has refractive index $n_1 = 1.65$. Layer 2 sits beneath it with index $n_2 = 1.42$. Layer 3 forms the base with index $n_3 = 1.15$. The light strikes the flat boundary interface between Layer 1 and Layer 2 at an angle of incidence equal to $\theta_1$. Show algebraically how to determine the critical bounding angle $\theta_1$ that causes the light ray to undergo Total Internal Reflection (TIR) exactly at the lower boundary interface between Layer 2 and Layer 3.

Detailed Analytical Path:

Step 1: Set up Snell's Law transitions across the internal layers:

$$\text{Boundary 1 to 2: } n_1 \sin\theta_1 = n_2 \sin\theta_2$$ $$\text{Boundary 2 to 3: } n_2 \sin\theta_2 = n_3 \sin\theta_3$$ By mathematical transitivity through the uniform parallel interfaces: $$n_1 \sin\theta_1 = n_3 \sin\theta_3$$

Step 2: Apply the physical criteria for Total Internal Reflection at the Layer 2/3 boundary:

For TIR to occur precisely at the interface of Layer 3, the refraction angle into Layer 3 must reach its limit: $\theta_3 = 90^\circ$. $$\sin\theta_3 = \sin(90^\circ) = 1$$

Step 3: Solve for the initial limiting angle $\theta_1$:

$$n_1 \sin\theta_1 = n_3 (1) \implies \sin\theta_1 = \frac{n_3}{n_1}$$ $$\sin\theta_1 = \frac{1.15}{1.65} \approx 0.69697$$ $$\theta_1 = \sin^{-1}(0.69697) \approx 44.18^\circ$$ Thus, any incident ray where $\theta_1 \ge 44.18^\circ$ guarantees complete structural reflection at the bottom layer interface.

Question 15 (2 Marks)

State the Principle of Superposition for mechanical waves and outline what happens to the total energy of a system during localized destructive cancellation nodes.

Analytical Response Path:
The Principle of Superposition states that when two or more waves overlap in a medium, the net resultant displacement equals the vector sum of the individual wave displacements at that point.
At a destructive node, localized displacement drops to zero, but net energy is not destroyed. Instead, energy is redistributed to adjacent regions of constructive interference, or stored momentarily as kinetic energy of the medium's particles, satisfying the law of conservation of energy.

Question 16 (2 Marks)

Define the Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics and describe its significance to how we measure temperature using standard scales.

Analytical Response Path:
The Zeroth Law states that if System A is in thermal equilibrium with System B, and System B is in thermal equilibrium with System C, then System A is in thermal equilibrium with System C.
This establishes a transitive property that allows us to define temperature as a valid physical property. It forms the physical basis for thermometers, allowing a calibrated tool (System B) to measure and compare temperatures across different environments.

Analytical Response Path:

Question 20 (4 Marks)

An open pipe resonating at its fundamental frequency produces a tone at $264\,\text{Hz}$. What is the new fundamental frequency if the pipe's physical length is cut down by exactly one-third of its original length?

Analytical Response Path:
The fundamental frequency of an open pipe is given by: $f_0 = \frac{v}{2L}$
Frequency is inversely proportional to length ($f \propto \frac{1}{L}$).
If the length is reduced by $\frac{1}{3}$, the new length is: $L' = L - \frac{1}{3}L = \frac{2}{3}L$
Therefore, the new frequency is: $$f' = \frac{v}{2(\frac{2}{3}L)} = \frac{3}{2} \left(\frac{v}{2L}\right) = \frac{3}{2} f_0$$ $$f' = 1.5 \times 264\,\text{Hz} = \mathbf{396\,\text{Hz}}$$
