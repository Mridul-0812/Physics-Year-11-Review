Module 1: Kinematics Workspace

An exhaustive, highly technical study of motion without considering the underlying forces or masses. This resource explores standard NESA Preliminary dot points, absolute algebraic and calculus derivations, graphical analysis layouts, relative frameworks, and rigorously evaluated laboratory precision conventions.

Syllabus Section 1.1

Data Precision: The Significant Figure Rule Matrix

In physical science, numbers recorded during experimental observations do not exist as absolute, infinite abstractions. They represent precise structural windows limited by the systemic resolution of laboratory instrumentation (such as vernier calipers, ticker-tape timers, and digital photogates). Consequently, tracing errors and rounding final calculations must comply with a standardized engineering framework.

The Least Significant Figures Mandate

When executing multiplication, division, or composite algebraic fractions within kinematics equations, the final calculated solution must be rounded directly to match the least number of significant figures provided in the initial problem inputs. This rule preserves physical reality, ensuring that an analytical conclusion never projects a false impression of precision beyond what the raw field measurements support.

The Low-Resolution Coarse Data Exception

Consider a situation where a vehicle's mass or displacement parameters are specified as a single unrefined integer, such as $50\text{ kg}$ or $300\text{ m}$. Strictly speaking, trailing zeros without a decimal point denote a single significant figure. However, rounding a highly granular, multi-stage kinematic sequence down to one single significant digit can severely degrade the structural value of the assessment item.

In these specific laboratory instances, the tracking metrics can be treated as an intentional approximation for exactly $5.0 \times 10^1\text{ kg}$ or $3.0 \times 10^2\text{ m}$. The analyst is permitted to preserve two or three significant figures throughout the operational workflow to prevent artificial computational degradation, provided that this structural decision is explicitly declared in the final written report.

Syllabus Section 1.2

Foundations of Vector Space vs. Scalar Fields

Kinematic systems require a clear distinction between properties that are independent of spatial orientation and those that change based on coordinate orientation.

  • Scalar Quantities Defined by a single scalar magnitude coupled to its respective measurement unit. They follow standard scalar arithmetic operations ($A + B = C$).
    Core Metrics: Time ($t$), Distance ($d$), Speed ($s$), Mass ($m$), Energy ($E$).
  • Vector Quantities Require a complete numeric magnitude, an appropriate unit, and an explicit spatial direction. They follow vector triangle addition rules and require geometric trigonometric resolutions.
    Core Metrics: Displacement ($\vec{s}$ or $\vec{x}$), Velocity ($\vec{v}$), Acceleration ($\vec{a}$), Force ($\vec{F}$).

Mathematical Definitions of Motion Parameters

We formalize linear kinematics by tracking position alterations along a single-dimension spatial coordinate axis system.

Distance ($d$)

The cumulative length of the actual path traversed by an object, regardless of direction. This value is always positive and monotonic for moving bodies: $$\Delta d \ge 0$$

Displacement ($s$ or $\Delta x$)

The net straight-line spatial vector extending from the initial baseline origin coordinate position ($x_i$) directly to the terminal endpoint position ($x_f$): $$\Delta x = x_f - x_i$$

Average Speed vs Velocity

Average speed tracks total distance over time, while average velocity tracks net vector displacement over time: $$\text{Speed}_{\text{avg}} = \frac{d_{\text{total}}}{\Delta t}$$ $$\vec{v}_{\text{avg}} = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} = \frac{x_f - x_i}{t_f - t_i}$$

Average Acceleration ($\vec{a}_{\text{avg}}$)

The time rate of change of the velocity vector. In uniform NESA applications, acceleration remains completely constant: $$\vec{a}_{\text{avg}} = \frac{\Delta \vec{v}}{\Delta t} = \frac{\vec{v}_f - \vec{v}_i}{t_f - t_i}$$

Extension Sub-Block: Continuous Calculus Tracking Concepts

As the observed time window ($\Delta t$) approaches zero, the average rates shrink to instantaneous properties. This limit marks the transition from algebra to differential calculus:

$$v(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} = \frac{dx}{dt}$$ $$a(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} = \frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{d^2x}{dt^2}$$

These continuous functions define instantaneous velocity as the first time-derivative of position, and instantaneous acceleration as the first time-derivative of velocity (or the second time-derivative of position).

Syllabus Section 1.3

Graphical Analysis of Uniform Linear Waveforms

Examining motion using visual graphs is a core skill evaluated in NESA examinations. Under the constraints of the regular syllabus, acceleration values are always completely constant. This means the lines on velocity graphs will always be straight, and the shapes formed under them will be simple geometric regions like rectangles and triangles.

Core Differences: Displacement-Time vs. Velocity-Time Graphs

Students frequently confuse the information provided by these two graph profiles. The key analytical features are detailed below:

Displacement-Time ($s\text{-}t$) Properties
  • Y-intercept: Shows the initial position coordinate ($s_0$) at time zero.
  • Gradient/Slope: Equals the instantaneous velocity ($v$). A steeper slope means a faster object.
  • Linear/Straight Line: Shows a constant, unchanging velocity ($a = 0$).
  • Horizontal Flat Line: The object is stationary ($v = 0$).
  • Area Under Curve: Holds no physical meaning in kinematic analysis.
Velocity-Time ($v\text{-}t$) Properties
  • Y-intercept: Shows the initial velocity parameter ($u$).
  • Gradient/Slope: Equals the uniform linear acceleration ($a$).
  • Linear Slope: Indicates that acceleration is completely constant.
  • Horizontal Flat Line: Acceleration is zero ($a = 0$), meaning the object travels at a constant velocity.
  • Area Under Curve: The geometric area bounded between the plot line and the zero-axis equals the net vector displacement ($\Delta x$).

Syllabus Section 1.4

Rigorously Derived SUVAT Core Formulations

When tracking motion with constant acceleration, we establish a core suite of predictive equations known as the SUVAT variables:
• $s$: net spatial displacement ($\text{m}$)    • $u$: initial velocity ($\text{m/s}$)    • $v$: final velocity ($\text{m/s}$)    • $a$: constant acceleration ($\text{m/s}^2$)    • $t$: elapsed time ($\text{s}$).

[Equation 1] — The Velocity Evolution Formula: $v = u + at$

A. Geometric/Algebraic Proof: By definition, uniform acceleration equals the constant gradient of a linear Velocity-Time graph: $$a = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} = \frac{v - u}{t - 0}$$ Multiplying both sides by the time variable $t$: $$at = v - u$$ Rearranging to isolate final velocity ($v$) yields Equation 1: $$v = u + at$$

B. Advanced Calculus Derivation: Define instantaneous acceleration as the time derivative of velocity: $$a = \frac{dv}{dt} \implies dv = a \cdot dt$$ Integrate both sides using definite boundary conditions (from $t=0, v=u$ to time $t, v$): $$\int_{u}^{v} 1 \, dv = \int_{0}^{t} a \, dt$$ Since acceleration ($a$) is constant, factor it out of the integration operator: $$[v]_{u}^{v} = a \int_{0}^{t} 1 \, dt \implies v - u = a[t]_{0}^{t}$$ $$v - u = a(t - 0) \implies v = u + at$$

[Equation 2] — The Spatial Displacement Formula: $s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$

A. Geometric/Algebraic Proof: The area bounded beneath a linear Velocity-Time graph represents displacement. This total geometry forms a trapezoid, which can be split into a lower rectangle and an upper triangle:
• Area of Lower Rectangle: $\text{width} \times \text{height} = t \times u = ut$
• Area of Upper Triangle: $\frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height} = \frac{1}{2} \times t \times (v - u)$
Summing these geometric regions: $$s = ut + \frac{1}{2}t(v - u)$$ From Equation 1, substitute $(v - u) = at$ into the triangle area expression: $$s = ut + \frac{1}{2}t(at) \implies s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$$

B. Advanced Calculus Derivation: Define instantaneous velocity as the first time derivative of position: $$v = \frac{ds}{dt} \implies ds = v \, dt$$ Substitute the time-dependent velocity relation derived in Equation 1 ($v = u + at$): $$ds = (u + at) \, dt$$ Integrate both sides using definite boundary conditions (from $t=0, s=0$ to time $t, s$): $$\int_{0}^{s} 1 \, ds = \int_{0}^{t} (u + at) \, dt$$ $$\int_{0}^{s} 1 \, ds = \int_{0}^{t} u \, dt + \int_{0}^{t} at \, dt$$ Since initial parameters $u$ and acceleration $a$ are uniform constants relative to time: $$s = u[t]_{0}^{t} + a\left[\frac{1}{2}t^2\right]_{0}^{t} \implies s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$$

[Equation 3] — The Time-Independent Formula: $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$

A. Geometric/Algebraic Proof: The displacement area can also be expressed directly using the area formula for a complete trapezoid: $$s = \left(\frac{u + v}{2}\right)t$$ Isolate chronological time $t$ from Equation 1 ($t = \frac{v - u}{a}$) and substitute it directly into the trapezoidal area expression: $$s = \left(\frac{v + u}{2}\right)\left(\frac{v - u}{a}\right)$$ Expand the numerator using the difference of two squares algebraic rule: $$s = \frac{v^2 - u^2}{2a} \implies 2as = v^2 - u^2 \implies v^2 = u^2 + 2as$$

B. Advanced Calculus Derivation: To derive this time-independent formulation using calculus, apply the chain rule to the definition of instantaneous acceleration: $$a = \frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{dv}{ds} \cdot \frac{ds}{dt}$$ Since $\frac{ds}{dt} = v$, substitute it directly into the product: $$a = v \cdot \frac{dv}{ds} \implies a \, ds = v \, dv$$ Perform definite integration across system boundaries from initial states to final values: $$\int_{0}^{s} a \, ds = \int_{u}^{v} v \, dv \implies a[s]_{0}^{s} = \left[\frac{1}{2}v^2\right]_{u}^{v}$$ $$as = \frac{1}{2}v^2 - \frac{1}{2}u^2 \implies 2as = v^2 - u^2 \implies v^2 = u^2 + 2as$$

Syllabus Section 1.5

Relative Velocity Vector Configurations

All physical observations are recorded relative to a specific frame of reference. If an observer at rest in frame $A$ tracks an object moving in frame $B$, we write the vector equation for relative velocity as:

$$\vec{v}_{AB} = \vec{v}_A - \vec{v}_B$$

Where $\vec{v}_{AB}$ represents the velocity of object $A$ relative to the frame of observer $B$. In collinear straight-line scenarios, vector subtraction simplifies to careful tracking of positive ($+$) and negative ($-$) signs.

Example 1: Multi-Stage Constant Acceleration Tracking

A maglev train car departs from a station, accelerating uniformly from rest at a rate of $2.50\text{ m/s}^2$ for exactly $6.0\text{ seconds}$. It then immediately applies its braking array, decelerating at a steady rate of $-1.2\text{ m/s}^2$ until it comes to a complete stop at the next platform. Calculate the total linear distance traveled by the car across both stages of its journey.

Step-by-Step Solution:

Stage 1 (Acceleration Phase):
Inputs: $u_1 = 0.0\text{ m/s}$, $a_1 = 2.50\text{ m/s}^2$, $t_1 = 6.0\text{ s}$.
First, determine the final velocity reached at the end of this phase ($v_1$): $$v_1 = u_1 + a_1 t_1 = 0 + (2.50)(6.0) = 15\text{ m/s}$$ Next, calculate the distance covered during this first interval ($s_1$): $$s_1 = u_1 t_1 + \frac{1}{2}a_1 t_1^2 = 0 + \frac{1}{2}(2.50)(6.0)^2 = 1.25 \times 36.0 = 45\text{ m}$$
Stage 2 (Braking Phase):
Inputs: $u_2 = 15\text{ m/s}$ (the final velocity of stage 1), $v_2 = 0.0\text{ m/s}$ (comes to a stop), $a_2 = -1.2\text{ m/s}^2$.
Use the time-independent equation to calculate the distance covered during braking ($s_2$): $$v_2^2 = u_2^2 + 2 a_2 s_2 \implies 0^2 = 15^2 + 2(-1.2)s_2$$ $$0 = 225 - 2.4 s_2 \implies 2.4 s_2 = 225 \implies s_2 = \frac{225}{2.4} = 93.75\text{ m}$$
Total Cumulative Distance Calculation & Significant Figures Check: $$s_{\text{total}} = s_1 + s_2 = 45\text{ m} + 93.75\text{ m} = 138.75\text{ m}$$ Looking at the initial inputs, $6.0\text{ s}$ and $-1.2\text{ m/s}^2$ both contain two significant figures. Round the final distance to match this level of precision: $$\mathbf{s_{\text{total}} = 1.4 \times 10^2\text{ m} \quad (\text{or } 140\text{ m rounded to 2 s.f.})}$$

Example 2: Complex Collinear Relative Intersection

An express passenger train travels along a straight track at a constant speed of $36.0\text{ m/s}$. Directly ahead on the same track, a slow-moving freight train is traveling in the same direction at a constant speed of $12.0\text{ m/s}$. The express driver suddenly notices the freight train when the distance between them is exactly $120.0\text{ meters}$. The driver immediately applies the brakes, causing a constant deceleration of $-2.00\text{ m/s}^2$. Determine if a collision occurs.

Step-by-Step Solution:

To simplify the problem, analyze the motion of the express train relative to the freight train's reference frame.

1. Determine relative starting parameters:
Initial relative velocity ($u_{\text{rel}}$) of the express train relative to the freight train: $$u_{\text{rel}} = v_{\text{express}} - v_{\text{freight}} = 36.0\text{ m/s} - 12.0\text{ m/s} = 24.0\text{ m/s}$$ Relative acceleration ($a_{\text{rel}}$) during braking: $$a_{\text{rel}} = a_{\text{express}} - a_{\text{freight}} = -2.00\text{ m/s}^2 - 0 = -2.00\text{ m/s}^2$$
2. Determine the minimum distance needed to stop relative to the freight train:
To avoid a collision, the express train must slow down until its relative velocity reaches zero ($v_{\text{rel}} = 0$) before covering the initial separation distance of $120.0\text{ m}$. $$v_{\text{rel}}^2 = u_{\text{rel}}^2 + 2 a_{\text{rel}} s_{\text{rel}}$$ $$0^2 = (24.0)^2 + 2(-2.00)s_{\text{stopping}}$$ $$0 = 576 - 4.00 s_{\text{stopping}} \implies 4.00 s_{\text{stopping}} = 576 \implies s_{\text{stopping}} = \frac{576}{4.00} = 144.0\text{ m}$$
3. Evaluate the Physical Outcome:
The express train requires a relative distance of $144.0\text{ meters}$ to match the speed of the freight train. Because the initial separation distance was only $120.0\text{ meters}$, the express train cannot stop in time. $$\mathbf{\text{Conclusion: A collision occurs.}}$$
