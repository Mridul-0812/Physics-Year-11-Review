Part 1: Detailed Exam-Style Exemplars

Review these 5 highly technical solutions to calibrate your mathematical formatting against top-tier NESA criteria.

Exemplar 1: Derivation of $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$ without Calculus

Question: Derive the time-independent constant acceleration kinematic formula $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$ using algebraic combinations of definitions for average acceleration and displacement.

Exemplar Solution:
1. Define uniform constant acceleration as the time rate of change of velocity: $$a = \frac{v - u}{t} \implies t = \frac{v - u}{a} \quad \text{[Equation 1]}$$ 2. Define net vector displacement under constant acceleration as the geometric average velocity multiplied by time: $$s = \left(\frac{u + v}{2}\right)t \quad \text{[Equation 2]}$$ 3. Substitute Equation 1 into Equation 2 to eliminate the time variable ($t$): $$s = \left(\frac{v + u}{2}\right)\left(\frac{v - u}{a}\right)$$ 4. Expand the numerator using the difference of two squares identity: $$s = \frac{v^2 - u^2}{2a}$$ 5. Rearrange to isolate final velocity squared ($v^2$): $$2as = v^2 - u^2 \implies v^2 = u^2 + 2as \quad \blacksquare$$

Exemplar 2: Multi-Vehicle Pursuit with Quadratic Systems

Question: A stationary police interceptor is passed by a speeding vehicle traveling at a constant velocity of $30.0\text{ m/s}$. The interceptor immediately accelerates from rest at a constant rate of $4.00\text{ m/s}^2$. Calculate the total distance the police car must travel to overtake the speeder.

Exemplar Solution:
1. Formulate the position equation for both vehicles relative to the launch point ($x = 0, t = 0$): $$s_{\text{speeder}} = v_{\text{constant}} \cdot t = 30.0t$$ $$s_{\text{police}} = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2 = 0 + \frac{1}{2}(4.00)t^2 = 2.00t^2$$ 2. Set the equations equal to represent the point of intercept ($s_{\text{speeder}} = s_{\text{police}}$): $$2.00t^2 = 30.0t \implies 2.00t^2 - 30.0t = 0$$ $$t(2.00t - 30.0) = 0$$ 3. Discard the trivial solution ($t = 0$) to solve for the intersection time ($t$): $$2.00t = 30.0 \implies t = 15.0\text{ seconds}$$ 4. Calculate the total pursuit distance by substituting the time back into either kinematic equation: $$s = 30.0 \times 15.0 = 450\text{ meters}$$ Both initial coordinates contain three significant figures, so the final verified answer is $\mathbf{450\text{ m}}$.

Exemplar 3: Collinear Multi-Stage Freight Braking

Question: A heavy locomotive traveling at $40.0\text{ m/s}$ applies intermediate service brakes for $10.0\text{ s}$, slowing down at a constant rate of $-1.50\text{ m/s}^2$. The driver then throws the emergency reverse-thruster array, increasing deceleration to $-4.00\text{ m/s}^2$ until the train stops. Find the total distance covered.

Exemplar Solution:
Stage 1 (Initial Service Braking): $$v_1 = u_1 + a_1 t_1 = 40.0 + (-1.50)(10.0) = 25.0\text{ m/s}$$ $$s_1 = u_1 t_1 + \frac{1}{2}a_1 t_1^2 = (40.0)(10.0) + \frac{1}{2}(-1.50)(10.0)^2 = 400 - 75.0 = 325\text{ m}$$ Stage 2 (Emergency Braking Phase):
Inputs: $u_2 = 25.0\text{ m/s}$, $v_2 = 0\text{ m/s}$, $a_2 = -4.00\text{ m/s}^2$. $$v_2^2 = u_2^2 + 2a_2 s_2 \implies 0^2 = (25.0)^2 + 2(-4.00)s_2$$ $$0 = 625 - 8.00s_2 \implies s_2 = \frac{625}{8.00} = 78.125\text{ m}$$ Total Cumulative Path: $$s_{\text{total}} = s_1 + s_2 = 325 + 78.125 = 403.125\text{ m}$$ Rounding to three significant figures to match the raw data gives $\mathbf{403\text{ m}}$.

Exemplar 4: Two-Dimensional Vector Boundary Crossings

Question: A ferry boat seeks to cross a straight river segment flowing due East at a constant speed of $3.00\text{ m/s}$. The ferry can travel at a maximum speed of $5.00\text{ m/s}$ through the water. Determine the angle relative to the bank at which the captain must steer the vessel to travel directly North across the channel.

Exemplar Solution:
1. Set up a vector addition triangle where the resultant velocity vector ($\vec{v}_{\text{resultant}}$) points straight North.
2. The downstream eastward current ($\vec{v}_{\text{river}} = 3.00\text{ m/s}$) forms the horizontal side, and the boat's speed through the water ($\vec{v}_{\text{boat}} = 5.00\text{ m/s}$) forms the hypotenuse pointing upstream (North-West).
3. Solve for the interior angle ($\theta$) relative to the true North axis using trigonometry: $$\sin(\theta) = \frac{\text{Opposite}}{\text{Hypotenuse}} = \frac{v_{\text{river}}}{v_{\text{boat}}} = \frac{3.00}{5.00} = 0.600$$ $$\theta = \arcsin(0.600) \approx 36.87^\circ \text{ West of North}$$ 4. Calculate the steering angle relative to the riverbank itself: $$\phi = 90.00^\circ + 36.87^\circ = 126.87^\circ \text{ upstream from the downstream bank orientation.}$$ Rounding to three significant figures yields $\mathbf{127^\circ}$.

Exemplar 5: The Low-Resolution Coarse Data Parameter Rule

Question: An object accelerates along a straight line at $6.0\text{ m/s}^2$ from rest over a distance recorded as $300\text{ m}$. Explain how the laboratory limitation rule applies when expressing the elapsed time with appropriate significant figures.

Exemplar Solution:
1. Use the standard displacement formula with $u = 0$: $$s = \frac{1}{2}at^2 \implies 300 = \frac{1}{2}(6.0)t^2 \implies 300 = 3.0t^2$$ $$t^2 = 100 \implies t = 10.0\text{ s}$$ 2. Under strict significant figure rules, the measurement "$300\text{ m}$" has only one significant figure, which would force the final answer to round to "$10\text{ s}$".
3. However, under the Coarse Data Exception Rule for laboratory reporting, intermediate values can assume an intended precision of two significant figures ($3.0 \times 10^2\text{ m}$) to preserve the usefulness of the calculation.
4. Therefore, the final baseline time is properly recorded as $\mathbf{10.\text{ s}}$ or $\mathbf{1.0 \times 10^1\text{ s}}$ to maintain two significant figures.

Question 8 (Quadratic Intersection — 4 Marks)

A runner with a 20.0-meter head start runs forward at a constant speed of $4.0\text{ m/s}$. A second runner starts from rest at the same origin point and pursues the first runner with a constant acceleration of $2.0\text{ m/s}^2$. Find the time required for the second runner to overtake the first.



View Solution

$s_1 = 20.0 + 4.0t$, $s_2 = \frac{1}{2}(2.0)t^2 = t^2$. Set equal: $t^2 - 4.0t - 20.0 = 0$. Using quadratic formula: $t = \frac{4.0 \pm \sqrt{(-4.0)^2 - 4(1)(-20.0)}}{2} = \frac{4.0 \pm \sqrt{16 + 80}}{2} = \frac{4.0 + 9.80}{2} = \mathbf{6.9\text{ s}}$.

Question 21 (Multi-Stage Graphical Kinematics — 8 Marks)

A high-performance prototype model train moves along a straight linear test track. Its motion over a $60\text{ s}$ window is monitored and plotted below as a piecewise continuous Velocity-Time graph, crossing both above and below the time axis.

(a) Determine the instantaneous acceleration of the train at $t = 25.0\text{ s}$. (2 Marks)
(b) State the distinct timestamp(s) or interval(s) where the train changes its directional heading. (1 Mark)
(c) Calculate the total distance covered by the train throughout the entire $60.0\text{ s}$ testing run. (3 Marks)
(d) Compute the magnitude of the final displacement from its initial origin at $t = 60.0\text{ s}$. (2 Marks)



View Solution
(a) Acceleration at $t = 25\text{ s}$ (Gradient of segment from $t=20$ to $t=32$):
$$a = \frac{v_f - v_i}{t_f - t_i} = \frac{-20 - 20}{32 - 20} = \frac{-40}{12} = \mathbf{-3.33\text{ m/s}^2}$$ [2 Marks]

(b) Direction changes (Time-axis intercepts where velocity flips signs):
• Intercept 1 lies exactly halfway along the linear segment between $(20, 20)$ and $(32, -20)$, which is at **$t = 26.0\text{ s}$**.
• Intercept 2 lies on the segment between $(44, -20)$ and $(52, 10)$. Slope is $\frac{10 - (-20)}{52 - 44} = \frac{30}{8} = 3.75\text{ m/s}^2$. Intercept: $0 = -20 + 3.75(t - 44) \implies t = 44 + 5.33 = \mathbf{49.33\text{ s}}$.
Therefore, changes direction at **$t = 26.0\text{ s}$ and $t = 49.33\text{ s}$** [1 Mark].

(c) Total Distance (Sum of absolute geometric regions under curve):
• Area 1 (Positive, $t=0$ to $26$): Trapezoid with bases $26$ and $10$, height $20 \implies A_1 = \frac{26+10}{2} \times 20 = 360\text{ m}$.
• Area 2 (Negative, $t=26$ to $49.33$): Trapezoid with bases $(49.33 - 26) = 23.33$ and $(44 - 32) = 12$, height $20 \implies A_2 = \frac{23.33+12}{2} \times 20 = 353.33\text{ m}$.
• Area 3 (Positive, $t=49.33$ to $60$): Triangle with base $(60 - 49.33) = 10.67$, height $10 \implies A_3 = \frac{1}{2} \times 10.67 \times 10 = 53.33\text{ m}$.
$$\text{Total Distance} = 360 + 353.33 + 53.33 = \mathbf{766.66\text{ m}}$$ [3 Marks].

(d) Magnitude of Net Displacement:
$$\vec{s} = A_1 - A_2 + A_3 = 360 - 353.33 + 53.33 = \mathbf{60.0\text{ m}\text{ [East/Forward]}}$$ [2 Marks].
Question 22 (Non-Uniform Relative Acceleration Interception — 4 Marks)

A sports car is idling at a red light. The instant the signal turns green, the car accelerates uniformly at $a_c = 4.50\text{ m/s}^2$. At that exact same fraction of a second, a commercial truck traveling at a fixed, constant velocity of $v_t = 24.0\text{ m/s}$ bypasses the car in the adjacent lane. Determine how far down the road the sports car will travel before it successfully overtakes the truck.



View Solution

Set up the kinematic position functions from the point of origin ($x_0 = 0$ at $t = 0$):
$$\text{Truck Position: } x_t(t) = v_t \cdot t = 24.0t$$ $$\text{Car Position: } x_c(t) = \frac{1}{2}a_c \cdot t^2 = \frac{1}{2}(4.50)t^2 = 2.25t^2$$ At interception, $x_c(t) = x_t(t)$:
$$2.25t^2 = 24.0t \implies t(2.25t - 24.0) = 0 \implies t = \frac{24.0}{2.25} = 10.67\text{ s}$$ Substitute time back into either position equation to evaluate displacement:
$$x = 24.0 \times 10.67 = \mathbf{256.0\text{ m}}$$

Question 23 (The Reaction-Deceleration Threshold Problem — 5 Marks)

A vehicle is cruising at $v_0 = 30.0\text{ m/s}$ when the driver spots an obstacle lying across the road $95.0\text{ m}$ ahead. The driver experiences a physiological human reaction delay of $\Delta t_{\text{react}} = 0.65\text{ s}$ before hitting the brakes. Once applied, the brakes generate a constant maximum deceleration rate of $a = -6.20\text{ m/s}^2$. Determine if the vehicle safely stops before hitting the obstacle, and calculate its remaining clearance or impact speed velocity.



View Solution

Step 1: Compute reaction distance traveled at constant velocity:
$$\Delta x_{\text{react}} = v_0 \cdot \Delta t_{\text{react}} = 30.0\text{ m/s} \times 0.65\text{ s} = 19.5\text{ m}$$ Remaining buffer distance left to stop: $95.0\text{ m} - 19.5\text{ m} = 75.5\text{ m}$.

Step 2: Calculate the physical braking stopping distance required:
Using $v^2 = u^2 + 2ax$ where terminal velocity $v = 0$:
$$0 = (30.0)^2 + 2(-6.20) \cdot \Delta x_{\text{brake}} \implies 0 = 900 - 12.4 \cdot \Delta x_{\text{brake}}$$ $$\Delta x_{\text{brake}} = \frac{900}{12.4} = 72.58\text{ m}$$
Conclusion: Total stopping track length required is $19.5 + 72.58 = 92.08\text{ m}$. Since $92.08\text{ m} < 95.0\text{ m}$, the car stops safely.
$$\text{Clearance Safety Margin} = 95.0\text{ m} - 92.08\text{ m} = \mathbf{2.92\text{ m}}$$

Question 24 (Asynchronous Free-Fall Intersect — 4 Marks)

Object A is dropped from rest from the top of an observation deck cliff of height $H = 120\text{ m}$. Exactly $1.50\text{ s}$ later, Object B is launched vertically upwards from the absolute base of the cliff with an initial velocity of $u_B = 45.0\text{ m/s}$. Neglecting air resistance and taking $g = 9.80\text{ m/s}^2$, calculate the height above the ground where these two items pass one another.



View Solution

Let $t$ represent the total time elapsed since Object A was released.
$$\text{Position of Object A (Height from ground): } y_A(t) = H - \frac{1}{2}gt^2 = 120 - 4.9t^2$$ Object B travels for time $(t - 1.50)$:
$$\text{Position of Object B: } y_B(t) = u_B(t - 1.5) - \frac{1}{2}g(t - 1.5)^2 = 45(t - 1.5) - 4.9(t - 1.5)^2$$ $$y_B(t) = 45t - 67.5 - 4.9(t^2 - 3t + 2.25) = 45t - 67.5 - 4.9t^2 + 14.7t - 11.025$$ $$y_B(t) = -4.9t^2 + 59.7t - 78.525$$ Set equations equal to find crossing intersection point: $y_A(t) = y_B(t)$:
$$120 - 4.9t^2 = -4.9t^2 + 59.7t - 78.525 \implies 120 = 59.7t - 78.525$$ $$59.7t = 198.525 \implies t = 3.325\text{ s}$$ Calculate height using Object A's clean profile:
$$y = 120 - 4.9(3.325)^2 = 120 - 54.17 = \mathbf{65.83\text{ m}}$$

Question 25 (The Consecutive Displacement Interval Challenge — 5 Marks)

A particle traveling under uniform acceleration along a straight coordinate line covers a distance of $d_1 = 42.0\text{ m}$ during an initial timed window of $\Delta t = 3.00\text{ s}$. It then continues onward to cover an additional distance of $d_2 = 90.0\text{ m}$ during the next consecutive interval of $\Delta t = 3.00\text{ s}$. Determine the particle's initial velocity $u$ at the very start of the first tracking period.



View Solution

Using the kinematic formula $s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$ for distinct overlapping intervals:
Interval 1 (First 3 seconds):
$$42.0 = u(3.00) + \frac{1}{2}a(3.00)^2 \implies 42.0 = 3u + 4.5a \implies 14.0 = u + 1.5a \quad \text{--- (Eq 1)}$$
Combined Interval (Total of first 6 seconds):
$$\text{Total distance } s_{\text{total}} = 42.0 + 90.0 = 132.0\text{ m}$$ $$132.0 = u(6.00) + \frac{1}{2}a(6.00)^2 \implies 132.0 = 6u + 18a \implies 22.0 = u + 3a \quad \text{--- (Eq 2)}$$
Subtract Equation 1 from Equation 2 to eliminate $u$:
$$(u + 3a) - (u + 1.5a) = 22.0 - 14.0 \implies 1.5a = 8.0 \implies a = \frac{8.0}{1.5} = 5.33\text{ m/s}^2$$ Substitute acceleration value back into Equation 1 to isolate initial velocity:
$$14.0 = u + 1.5(5.33) \implies 14.0 = u + 8.0 \implies u = \mathbf{6.00\text{ m/s}}$$

Question 26 (Asymmetric Vertical Boundary Challenge — 5 Marks)

A sound-monitoring drone is hovering at a steady height of $H$ meters above the edge of a deep vertical mine shaft. A structural bolt accidentally breaks loose from the chassis of the drone and drops from rest under gravity. Exactly $2.00\text{ s}$ after the bolt is dropped, the drone instantly begins an emergency dive straight down into the shaft with a constant downward acceleration of $a_d = 14.7\text{ m/s}^2$. If the drone manages to intercept the falling bolt at exactly the mouth level of the mine shaft ($y = 0$), calculate the initial hovering height $H$. (Take $g = 9.80\text{ m/s}^2$ and neglect air friction).



View Solution
Step 1: Set up the displacement equation for the bolt.
Let $t$ be the total time the bolt falls before being intercepted at the shaft entrance.
Since it falls from rest from height $H$ to ground level ($0$), its total vertical descent displacement is $H$ using $s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$:
$$H = \frac{1}{2}gt^2 = \frac{1}{2}(9.80)t^2 = 4.90t^2 \quad \text{--- (Eq 1)}$$
Step 2: Set up the displacement equation for the drone.
The drone travels the same vertical distance $H$, but it begins $2.00\text{ s}$ later. Therefore, its flight time is $(t - 2.00)$ seconds. It also starts from a hovering rest position ($u = 0$):
$$H = \frac{1}{2}a_d(t - 2.00)^2 = \frac{1}{2}(14.7)(t - 2.00)^2 = 7.35(t - 2.00)^2 \quad \text{--- (Eq 2)}$$
Step 3: Equate the two displacement representations to eliminate $H$.
$$4.90t^2 = 7.35(t - 2.00)^2$$ Divide both sides by $2.45$ to clean up the coefficients smoothly:
$$2t^2 = 3(t - 2.00)^2 \implies 2t^2 = 3(t^2 - 4t + 4)$$ $$2t^2 = 3t^2 - 12t + 12 \implies t^2 - 12t + 12 = 0$$
Step 4: Solve the quadratic expression.
$$t = \frac{-(-12) \pm \sqrt{(-12)^2 - 4(1)(12)}}{2(1)} = \frac{12 \pm \sqrt{144 - 48}}{2} = \frac{12 \pm \sqrt{96}}{2} = 6 \pm 2\sqrt{6}$$ $$\text{Evaluating solutions: } t_1 = 6 + 4.90 = 10.90\text{ s}, \quad t_2 = 6 - 4.90 = 1.10\text{ s}$$ Since the drone doesn't even begin moving until $t = 2.00\text{ s}$, the time must be greater than $2.00\text{ s}$. Thus, $t = 10.90\text{ s}$.

Step 5: Compute the original height $H$.
$$H = 4.90 \times (10.90)^2 \approx 4.90 \times 118.81 = \mathbf{582.2\text{ m}}$$
Question 27 (Kinematic Boundary Profile — 5 Marks)

A high-speed experimental research capsule is fired vertically straight downwards into a deep ocean research column with a non-zero initial speed of $8.50\text{ m/s}$. Upon contact with the water, fluid resistance introduces a constant upward deceleration rate of $2.20\text{ m/s}^2$ against its path. Calculate the exact time frame required for the capsule to first reach a total downward depth displacement of precisely $14.0\text{ m}$ within the column.



View Solution
Step 1: Coordinate sign conventions and tracking parameters.
Define down as the positive mechanical direction ($+$):
• Initial velocity: $u = +8.50\text{ m/s}$
• Acceleration: $a = -2.20\text{ m/s}^2$ (directed upwards, opposing velocity)
• Displacement: $s = +14.0\text{ m}$

Step 2: Apply the standard linear displacement equation ($s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$).
$$14.0 = 8.50t + \frac{1}{2}(-2.20)t^2$$ $$14.0 = 8.50t - 1.10t^2$$
Step 3: Rearrange terms to collect and solve for time ($t$).
$$1.10t^2 - 8.50t + 14.0 = 0$$
Using the quadratic equation formula ($t = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$):
$$t = \frac{-(-8.50) \pm \sqrt{(-8.50)^2 - 4(1.10)(14.0)}}{2(1.10)}$$ $$t = \frac{8.50 \pm \sqrt{72.25 - 61.60}}{2.20} = \frac{8.50 \pm \sqrt{10.65}}{2.20}$$ $$\sqrt{10.65} \approx 3.263$$
Step 4: Identify individual roots and isolate the physically matching timestamp.
$$t_1 = \frac{8.50 + 3.263}{2.20} = \frac{11.763}{2.20} = 5.35\text{ s}$$ $$t_2 = \frac{8.50 - 3.263}{2.20} = \frac{5.237}{2.20} = 2.38\text{ s}$$
Physical Context Interpretation: Both roots yield valid mathematical configurations, implying the vehicle passes the $14.0\text{ m}$ depth mark twice. It crosses down past this distance at **$2.38\text{ s}$**, decelerates completely to rest at a deeper position, and is then accelerated backwards up the column by the fluid buoyant force profiles, returning to cross the $14.0\text{ m}$ line from below at $5.35\text{ s}$. Because the prompt specifies finding the time frame to first reach this displacement, select the initial chronological event: $t = \mathbf{2.38\text{ s}}$.
