Exemplar Problem 1

Question: Derive the algebraic relationship for the net electrical resistance of two resistors $R_1$ and $R_2$ arranged in a parallel layout network. Show your step-by-step logic explicitly using standard Kirchhoff conservation laws without calculus primitives.

Step-by-Step Mathematical Solution and Proof:

1. Let two resistors $R_1$ and $R_2$ be tied directly between two single electrical junctions (nodes) $A$ and $B$.

2. According to Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL), which states the conservation of energy per unit charge along any closed structural loop pathway, the potential drop across each branching resistor element must be equal to the overarching system potential drop $V$:

$$V_{\text{total}} = V_1 = V_2 = V$$

3. According to Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL), which asserts the absolute conservation of electric charge carriers passing a junction point per unit time, the total systemic current input $I$ entering node $A$ must equal the sum of the discrete currents split into each available trace branch:

$$I_{\text{total}} = I_1 + I_2$$

4. Applying Ohm's Law in its current form ($I = \frac{V}{R}$) to replace the current definitions for each branch yields:

$$\frac{V}{R_{\text{parallel}}} = \frac{V}{R_1} + \frac{V}{R_2}$$

5. Because the scalar voltage potential factor $V$ is uniform throughout every term by rule of KVL, we divide it out cleanly from both sides of the structural equality expression to yield the absolute core parallel identity relation:

$$\frac{1}{R_{\text{parallel}}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2}$$
Exemplar Problem 2

Question: An alpha particle ($q = +3.204 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$) is injected into a uniform constant electric field region formed between two parallel plates spaced precisely $d = 5.00 \text{ mm}$ apart. If the steady potential difference across the dynamic array is $V = 2500 \text{ V}$, calculate the structural vector field intensity and the total mechanical force experienced by the carrier.

Step-by-Step Mathematical Solution and Proof:

1. First, transform the metric spacing parameter down to standard SI length units: $d = 5.00 \text{ mm} = 5.00 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m}$.

2. Calculate the electric field strength ($E$) inside the parallel boundaries using the linear potential field expression derived in Module 4 theory:

$$E = \frac{V}{d} = \frac{2500 \text{ V}}{5.00 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m}} = 5.00 \times 10^5 \text{ V/m (or N/C)}$$

3. Calculate the mechanical force scalar magnitude ($F$) acting directly on the alpha particle using the electric force relation definition:

$$F = qE = (3.204 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}) \times (5.00 \times 10^5 \text{ N/C}) = 1.602 \times 10^{-13} \text{ N}$$
Exemplar Problem 3

Question: A long, straight power calibration line carries an active current of $I = 24.0 \text{ A}$. Calculate the magnetic flux density ($B$) at a perpendicular radial measurement coordinate situated exactly $r = 12.0 \text{ cm}$ away from the core axis center line.

Step-by-Step Mathematical Solution and Proof:

1. Normalize the geometric radial distance input down to standard SI meters: $r = 12.0 \text{ cm} = 0.120 \text{ m}$.

2. State the primary formula derived via the Biot-Savart mechanism for the exterior magnetic field lines tracking around an infinitely long straight conductor wire:

$$B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r}$$

3. Substitute the fundamental permeability constant of free space ($\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7} \text{ T}\cdot\text{m/A}$) alongside the operational current value:

$$B = \frac{(4\pi \times 10^{-7} \text{ T}\cdot\text{m/A}) \times 24.0 \text{ A}}{2\pi \times 0.120 \text{ m}}$$

4. Execute algebraic cancellation of the common factor $2\pi$ to streamline the numeric execution steps:

$$B = \frac{2 \times 10^{-7} \times 24.0}{0.120} = \frac{4.80 \times 10^{-6}}{0.120} = 4.00 \times 10^{-5} \text{ Tesla (T)}$$
Exemplar Problem 4

Question: State the exact law of physical conservation that underpins Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL), and prove its structural consistency by analyzing a simple loop network with a DC source $V_s$ powering a linear series pair of loads $R_1$ and $R_2$.

Step-by-Step Mathematical Solution and Proof:

1. Kirchhoff's Voltage Law is underpinned by the immutable Law of Conservation of Energy. Electric potential difference is explicitly defined as the work executed or electrical potential energy transferred per unit charge:

$$V = \frac{W}{q}$$

2. In a closed loop configuration tracking around an entire electrical system mesh layout, any static point location has a single, definite electro-potential energy value. Therefore, if a test charge carrier completes an entire closed loop sequence and returns to its exact starting point, its net change in potential energy ($\Delta W$) must equal exactly zero.

$$\Delta W_{\text{net}} = 0 \implies q \sum \Delta V = 0 \implies \sum \Delta V = 0$$

3. For a series track containing a source input ($V_s$) and two loads, the energy input equals the sum of the energy drops: $V_s - I R_1 - I R_2 = 0$, proving complete structural compliance with thermodynamic laws.

Section 2: Multiple Choice Questions (MCQs)

Question 1 (MCQ)

A physical system consists of an insulated copper sphere containing an initial net charge of $-6.4 \times 10^{-15} \text{ C}$. If an engineering operation successfully extracts $15,000$ electrons from this sphere, what is the final net residual charge status of the object?

Correct Response: b

Extracting electrons removes negative charge, making the sphere less negative. The total charge removed is:

$$\Delta q = (-15000) \times (-1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}) = +2.403 \times 10^{-15} \text{ C}$$ $$q_{\text{final}} = q_{\text{initial}} + \Delta q = -6.4 \times 10^{-15} \text{ C} + 2.4 \times 10^{-15} \text{ C} = -4.0 \times 10^{-15} \text{ C}$$
Question 2 (MCQ)

Two isolated point charges are separated by a distance $r$ in a vacuum. If the magnitude of each charge is doubled and the separation distance between them is also doubled, the electrostatic force between them changes by what factor?

Correct Response: a

By Coulomb's Law, the proportional tracking model is:

$$F_{\text{new}} \propto \frac{(2q_1)(2q_2)}{(2r)^2} = \frac{4 \cdot q_1 q_2}{4 \cdot r^2} = F_{\text{initial}}$$
Question 3 (MCQ)

Which statement correctly describes the geometric orientation of electric field lines and equipotential surfaces across any arbitrary electrostatic field structure?

Correct Response: c

Because moving a charge along an equipotential path requires zero net work ($\Delta V = 0$), the electric force vector must always point at a right angle ($90^\circ$) to that path, forcing field lines to intersect equipotential contours perpendicularly.

Question 4 (MCQ)

An electrical current of $I = 4.00 \text{ A}$ is driven through a uniform resistor component for a runtime window of $t = 2.50 \text{ minutes}$. What is the net quantity of scalar charge passing through the device during this window?

Correct Response: b

Convert minutes to SI seconds: $t = 2.50 \times 60 = 150 \text{ s}$. Apply current rate formula:

$$q = I \cdot t = 4.00 \text{ A} \times 150 \text{ s} = 600 \text{ C}$$
Question 5 (MCQ)

An incandescent lamp filament is tested under adjustable voltage constraints and exhibits a non-linear $I\text{-}V$ trace curve that bends away from the current axis at high values. This non-ohmic behaviour is primarily caused by which macroscopic parameter shift?

Correct Response: b

As voltage increases, the increased collision frequency of charge carriers transfers energy to the metallic lattice, increasing its temperature. These thermal lattice vibrations increase the collision rate of flowing electrons, causing a non-linear increase in internal resistance.

Question 6 (MCQ)

Three identical resistors are wired together in a parallel configuration matrix. If the equivalent resistance of this combined network is measured as $R_p = 4.0\ \Omega$, what is the individual resistance value of each standalone component?

Correct Response: c

$$\frac{1}{R_p} = \frac{1}{R} + \frac{1}{R} + \frac{1}{R} = \frac{3}{R} \implies R = 3 \cdot R_p = 3 \times 4.0\ \Omega = 12.0\ \Omega$$
Question 7 (MCQ)

An ideal ammeter and an ideal voltmeter are connected into a circuit network to collect operational baseline values. How must they be placed relative to the target test load?

Correct Response: b

An ammeter measures the flow rate through a loop and must be placed in series; it has near-zero internal resistance to avoid altering the circuit. A voltmeter measures the difference in potential energy between two nodes and must be placed in parallel; it has near-infinite internal resistance to prevent current leakage through the meter.

Question 8 (MCQ)

What fundamental structural outcome occurs if a permanent bar magnet is mechanically cut into two equal physical halves across its central longitudinal symmetry line?

Correct Response: b

Isolated magnetic monopoles do not exist. Cutting a magnet exposes new poles at the fracture boundaries because the underlying microscopic atomic dipoles remain oriented in their original direction.

Question 10 (MCQ)

An engineering system requires a custom solenoid to produce an internal magnetic field strength of $B = 6.28 \times 10^{-3} \text{ T}$ using a constant current of $I = 2.0 \text{ A}$. What linear turn density ($n = \frac{N}{L}$) must the winding design possess to hit this target parameter?

Correct Response: a

Question 9 (SAQ)

State Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL) and explain how it directly relates to the law of conservation of electric charge at any circuit junction coordinate.

Expected Response Framework:

Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL) states that the total current entering any junction point must exactly equal the total current leaving that same junction. Because current is the rate of charge flow ($I = \frac{\Delta q}{\Delta t}$), KCL ensures that electric charge cannot accumulate or vanish at a junction, adhering strictly to the Law of Conservation of Charge.

Question 11 (SAQ)

An engineering solenoid with a longitudinal length of $L = 25.0 \text{ cm}$ contains a total of $N = 600$ wire loops. Determine the current ($I$) required to generate an internal core magnetic field strength of $B = 3.00 \times 10^{-3} \text{ T}$.

Question 12 (SAQ)

Define the term ferromagnetic and explain how magnetic domain spaces behave when a piece of unmagnetized soft iron is brought into a strong external magnetic field structure.

Expected Response Framework:

Ferromagnetic materials (such as iron, nickel, or cobalt) contain atoms with unpaired electron spins that create strong localized magnetic dipoles. In an unmagnetized state, these magnetic domains point in random directions, canceling each other out macroscopically. When exposed to an external magnetic field, these domains align along the external field vector and expand, creating a strong net macroscopic induced magnetisation.

Question 20 (SAQ) [Comprehensive Multi-Part Synthesis Problem]

An engineering test circuit consists of a parallel pair of resistors ($R_1 = 6.00\ \Omega$ and $R_2 = 3.00\ \Omega$) connected in series with a single standalone resistor ($R_3 = 4.00\ \Omega$). This complete circuit load is connected across a power pack outputting a stable $V = 12.0 \text{ V}$.
